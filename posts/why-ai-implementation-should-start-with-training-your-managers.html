Why AI Implementation Should Start With Training Your Managers

14 May 2026 5 min read By Marcela

Most organisations that roll out AI tools follow the same pattern. They pick a tool, buy some licences, send a quick announcement, and wait to see what happens. A few enthusiastic early adopters start using it. Everyone else carries on as before. After three months, nothing much has changed — except that the company is now paying a monthly subscription for something most of its team ignores.

The organisations that get real value from AI do something different. They do not start with the tools. They start with the managers.

Managers are the multiplier

A manager's relationship with AI does not just affect their own work. It shapes the behaviour of everyone who reports to them.

If a manager is sceptical about AI, their team will quietly shelve whatever tools were announced. If a manager is enthusiastic but uninformed, their team will start using AI without any guardrails — and eventually something will go wrong with a client document, a piece of sensitive data, or a decision that should have had a human check it. If a manager understands both the potential and the risks, they create an environment where AI is used thoughtfully, consistently, and in ways that actually improve how the team works.

Training staff without training managers is like putting up road signs and not telling anyone they exist. The message reaches people, but there is no one to reinforce it when reality gets complicated.

The gap that most organisations miss

When AI goes wrong in an organisation, the root cause is rarely a bad tool. It is almost always a gap in judgment — someone who did not know where the line was, or someone who knew but did not feel empowered to pause and check.

Managers are the people who set the tone for both of those things. They decide — explicitly or by example — what counts as an acceptable use of AI. They are the ones a team member approaches when they are not sure whether something is okay. They are the ones who either notice when AI output has not been checked, or do not notice.

If managers have not been trained, those moments of uncertainty resolve themselves in whatever direction feels most convenient. That is when data ends up in the wrong tool, when AI-generated content goes to a client without a proper review, and when small problems accumulate into the kind of incident that is genuinely difficult to explain.

What the EU AI Act adds to this picture

Article 4 of the EU AI Act requires that organisations deploying AI ensure their staff have a level of AI literacy appropriate to their role. That obligation sits most heavily on people who are making decisions informed by AI — which, in most organisations, means managers.

A manager who is approving reports generated with AI assistance, directing how their team uses AI tools, or relying on AI-produced summaries to inform decisions is not a passive bystander to AI use. They are an active participant in it. The EU AI Act treats them accordingly.

This is not an abstract legal point. If something goes wrong and a regulator asks how an organisation managed its AI use, the answer "we sent an email to staff" will not be adequate. What will be adequate is evidence that the people responsible for oversight — managers — understood what they were overseeing.

What manager AI training should actually cover

Effective AI training for managers is not the same as general staff training scaled up. It needs to address the specific decisions managers make.

How AI tools actually work — and where they fail. Managers do not need a technical deep dive. They do need to understand that AI can produce confident-sounding outputs that are factually wrong, that it has no understanding of your organisation's context unless you provide it, and that the quality of what you get depends heavily on the quality of what you ask. Without this baseline, a manager cannot meaningfully evaluate AI-generated work.

Data classification and what stays out of public tools. Managers are often the ones approving workflows. If they do not understand why certain data should not go into a free AI tool — client names, financial information, HR records, commercially sensitive strategy — they cannot make good decisions about which processes are appropriate for AI and which are not.

Human oversight in practice, not just in principle. The EU AI Act's requirement for human oversight sounds straightforward, but in practice it requires judgment. Managers need to understand what meaningful review of AI output looks like — not just "I read it" but "I checked the claims that mattered, I took responsibility for what went out." They also need to model this behaviour visibly so that their teams do the same.

How to handle uncertainty — and how to create space for it. One of the most important things a manager can do is make it safe for a team member to say "I am not sure if this is okay." If the culture is one where pausing is seen as a sign of incompetence, people will not pause. Managers set that culture. Training helps them set it intentionally.

The sequence that works

The pattern that tends to produce the best outcomes is straightforward. Train managers first. Give them time to sit with the material, ask questions, and think about how it applies to their team. Then roll out broader staff training — with managers who are ready to reinforce it, answer questions, and be consistent in how they respond to edge cases.

This sequence does something that a single all-staff training event cannot: it builds institutional knowledge at the level where it can actually be applied. The manager becomes a resource, not just another attendee.

It also changes the dynamics of the training itself. When staff know that their manager has already been through this, that the organisation takes it seriously enough to train leadership first, the message lands differently. It is not just a compliance exercise. It is something the organisation is genuinely invested in getting right.

A practical note for Irish SMEs

For smaller organisations — where a manager might be a team lead with five direct reports, or a business owner who doubles as their own operations manager — the principle is the same, even if the structure looks different.

The question is not "who has the title of manager?" It is "who is responsible for how this team works, what it produces, and whether the right checks are in place?" That person needs to understand AI well enough to make good decisions about it. Not perfectly. Not technically. But well enough to know when to proceed, when to pause, and when to ask a question.

That level of understanding does not come from a terms-of-service page or an all-staff email. It comes from training that is specific, practical, and connected to the actual work your organisation does.
