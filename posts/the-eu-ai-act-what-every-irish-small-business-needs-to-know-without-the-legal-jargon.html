The EU AI Act: What Every Irish Small Business Needs to Know (Without the Legal Jargon)

4 May 2026 4 min read By Marcela

The EU AI Act came into force in August 2024. Most Irish small businesses have not heard of it, or have heard of it and assumed it is something for large technology companies to worry about. Both assumptions are understandable. Both are wrong.

If your business uses AI tools in any commercial context — and at this point, most do — the EU AI Act creates obligations for you. Some of those obligations are already in effect. Others come into force over the next two years. The good news is that for most small businesses, getting to grips with the basics is not as complicated as it sounds.

What the EU AI Act actually is

The EU AI Act is the world's first comprehensive legal framework for artificial intelligence. It applies across all EU member states — including Ireland — and covers organisations that develop, deploy, or use AI systems commercially.

Its core logic is a risk-based approach. AI systems that could cause serious harm to people — systems used in hiring, credit scoring, law enforcement, critical infrastructure — face the strictest requirements. AI systems that are lower risk face lighter obligations. Most of the AI used by Irish small businesses falls into the lower-risk categories, but that does not mean it falls outside the Act entirely.

The obligation that applies to almost everyone: Article 4

Article 4 of the EU AI Act requires organisations to ensure that staff who use AI in their work have a sufficient level of AI literacy — meaning they understand how AI works, what its limitations are, and how to use it responsibly in their specific role.

This obligation is already in force. It applies regardless of the size of your organisation. If your receptionist uses an AI scheduling tool, if your accountant uses AI to process invoices, if your marketing team uses AI to draft content — those staff members need a level of AI literacy appropriate to what they are doing.

What does that look like in practice? It does not require a formal certification. It means people understanding the difference between a safe tool and an unsafe one for a given type of data. It means knowing that AI can be wrong and checking the outputs that matter. It means being aware that some AI use carries data protection implications. A solid one-hour training session covers most of it. But the organisation needs to be able to show it has done something, not just assumed everything is fine.

The risk tiers — where does your AI use sit?

Beyond Article 4, the EU AI Act categorises AI systems into four broad tiers.

Unacceptable risk — systems that are banned outright. These include AI used for social scoring by public authorities, real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces, and systems that exploit psychological vulnerabilities to manipulate behaviour. If you are a small business, you are almost certainly not in this territory.

High risk — systems that affect important decisions about people. Examples include AI used in hiring and recruitment, credit scoring, educational assessment, access to essential services, and certain safety-critical applications. If your business uses AI to inform decisions in any of these areas, you have more significant obligations: documentation, human oversight, transparency, and regular review. Most small businesses are not in this category either, but it is worth checking.

Limited risk — systems that interact with people in ways that require transparency. If you use a chatbot to communicate with customers, or if AI is generating content that is presented as human-produced, you may have disclosure obligations. Customers have a right to know when they are talking to an AI.

Minimal risk — most general-purpose AI tools used internally fall here. Drafting documents, summarising text, generating images for internal use, using AI to help with research. These carry the lightest requirements, but Article 4 still applies.

What this means for a typical Irish SME

For most small businesses, the EU AI Act means three practical things right now.

First, make sure your team has basic AI literacy. You do not need a university course. You need your staff to understand enough to use AI tools responsibly — and you need to be able to show you have done this if anyone ever asks.

Second, know which tools you are using and what they do with data. Some AI tools are appropriate for business use. Others are not — particularly for anything involving personal data about clients, employees, or EU residents generally. The distinction matters for both the EU AI Act and for GDPR, which continues to apply independently.

Third, if you are using AI in any process that affects decisions about people — including your own staff — think carefully about whether that sits in the high-risk category and what that means for how you manage it.

The question to ask yourself this week

Here is the simplest diagnostic: if a regulator asked you today to explain how your organisation manages its AI use, could you give a clear answer?

If the answer is no — if you are not sure which tools are in use, do not have any written guidelines, and have not done any staff training — that is where to start. Not with a large compliance project, but with the basics: find out what is actually happening, write down the rules, and make sure your team understands them.

That is the whole of it, for most small businesses. It is not complicated. It just needs to be done.
