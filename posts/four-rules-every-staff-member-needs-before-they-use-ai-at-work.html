Four Rules Every Staff Member Needs Before They Use AI at Work

14 May 2026 4 min read By Marcela

Before the policy documents. Before the compliance checklists. Before anyone talks about the EU AI Act or GDPR or risk tiers — there are four things every person in your organisation needs to understand the first time they open an AI tool for work.

These are not rules written by lawyers. They are habits. The kind that, once they are in your head, run quietly in the background every time you use AI at work. The organisations that avoid expensive AI mistakes are usually not the ones with the longest policies. They are the ones where staff have internalised a small number of simple principles.

Here they are.

1. Know what kind of room you are in

Not all AI tools are the same. The most important distinction is not which tool you are using, but where your data goes when you use it.

A free, public AI tool — a standard ChatGPT account, a free version of any generative AI — operates a bit like a conversation in a public café. You are not the only one there. What you share may be used to train future versions of the model. The terms of service say so, usually in small print.

A paid enterprise tool — Microsoft Copilot integrated with your Microsoft 365 subscription, for example — operates more like a private office. Your inputs stay within your organisation's environment. They are not shared with the outside world or used to train public models.

Before you type anything work-related into an AI tool, ask yourself: do I know which kind of room I am in? If you are not certain, assume it is the café. Behave accordingly.

2. The newspaper test

This one is simple enough to remember in the moment, which is the point.

Before you paste something into an AI tool, ask: would I be comfortable if this text appeared in a newspaper tomorrow, next to the name of the AI tool I am using?

If the answer is no — if it contains a client's name, a financial figure, a legal matter, a strategic decision, a staff member's personal circumstances — it stays out. Full stop.

This is not about distrusting AI. It is about recognising that the data you input goes somewhere, is processed somewhere, and in some tools is stored somewhere. The newspaper test is a fast, memorable shortcut to catching the moments when habit is about to override judgment.

The things that most commonly fail this test: meeting notes with client names in them, contract drafts with commercially sensitive terms, performance feedback about named individuals, anything marked confidential in any other context.

3. AI is a capable assistant, not a reliable authority

AI tools are genuinely impressive at drafting, summarising, explaining, and generating. They are not reliable at being right.

The technical term is hallucination — when an AI produces confident, fluent, plausible-sounding output that is factually wrong. It happens regularly, across every major AI tool, on topics ranging from legislation to company names to mathematical calculations. The problem is not that AI gets things wrong. The problem is that it does not signal when it is getting things wrong. It sounds exactly the same whether it is accurate or not.

This has practical consequences. An AI-generated summary of a legal document might miss a key clause. An AI-produced figure in a financial report might be wrong. An AI-drafted email to a client might contain an error of fact about your own services.

The rule is: treat AI output the way you would treat work from a capable but inexperienced colleague. Review it. Check the claims that matter. Take responsibility for what you send. The AI produced a draft. You are producing the final version.

This is also, incidentally, a legal obligation. The EU AI Act requires human oversight of AI-assisted decisions — especially ones that affect other people.

4. AI does not know your organisation — you have to tell it

A public AI tool has no idea who you are, who your clients are, what your organisation values, or what is appropriate in your specific context. It has been trained on an enormous amount of human-generated text, but none of it was about your business specifically.

This matters in two ways.

The first is accuracy. If you ask AI to draft a client email without giving it any context about who the client is, what the relationship is like, or what you are trying to achieve, you will get a generic output that may or may not be suitable. The more context you provide — the purpose, the audience, the tone, any constraints — the more useful the result.

The second is risk. AI generating content without context can produce outputs that are technically correct but culturally wrong for your organisation — the wrong tone for a sensitive client relationship, a claim that is accurate in general but not accurate for your specific services, a level of formality that does not match how you communicate. None of these are catastrophic on their own, but they are the kind of thing that erodes trust with clients and colleagues over time.

The habit is to treat AI like a very capable new hire on their first day. Brief them properly. Give them the context they need. Check the result before it goes anywhere.

The short version

If you need to pass these on to a colleague in thirty seconds:

Know whether your AI tool is public or private. If you are not sure, treat it as public. Do not put in anything that would embarrass you or your organisation if it went public. Check everything AI produces before you use it. Give it enough context to do a good job, and take responsibility for the output.

That is the whole thing. Everything else — the compliance frameworks, the policies, the legal obligations — builds on top of these four habits. But the habits come first.
