The Real Risks of Using AI Blindly: A Wake-Up Call for Irish Small Businesses

5 May 2026 4 min read By Marcela

AI tools are genuinely useful. They can draft emails in seconds, summarise long documents, answer routine customer queries, and help a small team punch well above its weight. If you are not using them at all, your competitors probably are.

But there is a version of AI use that creates more problems than it solves — and it is far more common than most business owners realise. It is not dramatic or malicious. It is just unchecked. And unchecked AI use carries five specific risks that every Irish small business should understand before they go any further.

Risk 1: Hallucinations — when AI confidently gets it wrong

Every major AI tool, including the most capable and expensive ones, produces hallucinations. That is the term for when AI generates content that is fluent, confident, and plausible-sounding — but factually wrong.

AI does not know when it is uncertain. It does not flag guesses. It writes "the regulation came into force on 1 August 2024" with exactly the same tone it would use for something it knows with certainty. If you are relying on AI to produce client-facing content, legal summaries, financial data, or technical documentation, and you are not checking the outputs carefully, some of what you send will be wrong.

The practical protection is simple but requires discipline: treat every AI output as a capable first draft, not a finished product. Someone with the relevant knowledge needs to review it before it goes anywhere that matters.

Risk 2: Privacy — where does your data actually go?

When you paste a paragraph into a free AI tool, that text goes somewhere. Under the terms of service of most free generative AI tools, it may be stored, reviewed, and in some cases used to train future versions of the model. This is not a scandal — it is in the small print that almost nobody reads.

The problem arises when the text being pasted contains personal data. A client's name and the detail of their situation. An employee's performance review. A commercially sensitive contract. Under GDPR, transferring personal data about EU residents to services hosted in the United States requires specific legal safeguards. Most free AI tools do not provide them, and most people using those tools do not know this.

The rule of thumb that protects most organisations: if you would not be comfortable seeing that text in a newspaper next to the name of the AI tool you used, it should not go in.

Risk 3: Transparency — your clients may have a right to know

Depending on how AI is used in your business, there are circumstances where transparency becomes a legal or ethical obligation rather than a choice.

If AI is generating communications that go to clients under your name, producing content that influences decisions affecting individuals, or being used in a way that clients would reasonably want to know about, the question of whether to disclose is not just ethical — it may be required. The EU AI Act sets transparency requirements for certain categories of AI use. The specifics depend on the context, but the direction of travel is clear: the era of using AI invisibly is ending.

Starting now from a position of openness — being willing to tell clients when and how AI is involved in your work — is both lower risk and increasingly what clients expect.

Risk 4: Bias — AI reflects the data it was trained on

AI models are trained on vast amounts of human-generated text. That text reflects the biases, assumptions, and gaps of whoever produced it. When AI is used to draft job advertisements, screen applications, generate customer-facing recommendations, or produce communications that affect different groups differently, those embedded biases can surface in ways that are difficult to spot and potentially discriminatory.

This risk is particularly acute in hiring, customer service, and any process that involves making judgments about people. It does not require bad intent — it just requires not looking closely enough at what the AI is actually producing.

The protection is the same as for hallucinations: human review, by people who know what to look for, before AI-generated outputs are used for anything consequential.

Risk 5: Brand trust — once it goes wrong, it is hard to fix

The risks above are all, ultimately, risks to your reputation. A client who receives incorrect information generated by AI. A staff member whose confidential details end up in an inappropriate tool. A customer who discovers that something presented as your own work was produced without any meaningful human involvement.

For small businesses, reputation is everything. You do not have a PR team to manage a crisis. You do not have the legal resources to fight a GDPR enforcement action easily. The cost of an AI-related incident — whether that is a client complaint, a data breach notification, or simply the loss of trust from someone who expected more — is proportionally much higher for a small organisation than for a large one.

The good news is that this risk is almost entirely preventable. Not by avoiding AI, but by using it with basic awareness of where the lines are.

What prevention actually looks like

None of this requires a large compliance project. The foundation is straightforward:

  • Know which tools your team is using and what those tools do with data
  • Have a clear rule about what data stays out of public AI tools
  • Build a check into any process where AI output goes to a client or affects a decision
  • Make sure your team understands the difference between a public AI tool and an enterprise one

That is not a compliance framework. It is common sense, made explicit. The organisations that get into trouble with AI are rarely the ones that tried to be responsible and fell slightly short. They are the ones that never thought about it at all.
